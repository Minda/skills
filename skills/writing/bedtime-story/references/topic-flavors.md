# Topic Flavors

Load this file when picking the angle and tone for a specific topic. Each genre has a slightly different relationship between "substance" and "drift."

## Natural history and science

**Examples**: octopuses, mycelium networks, ocean currents, glaciation, the moon's recession, slime molds, the carbon cycle, deep-sea hydrothermal vents.

**Why it works at bedtime**: scale dwarfs the listener's daily worries. Geologic and biological time frames are inherently slowing.

**Act II shape**: layer surprising structural facts. The story is allowed to wonder. "And then it turns out…" is a useful phrase. Don't rush the surprise.

**Voice to avoid**: textbook explainer, lecture, "Did you know…" The tone is closer to a friend who knows a lot about this and is thinking aloud at a slow pace.

**Sample opening**: *"Tonight we are going to spend a while with the octopus. Not any particular octopus. Just the octopus, as a kind of being in the world."*

## History

**Examples**: the Hanseatic League, ancient libraries, the Silk Road as daily life, the postal system, the spice trade, monastery life in the early Middle Ages, the slow building of cathedrals, the history of bread.

**Why it works at bedtime**: long time horizons. Most listeners' lives are measured in decades. A story measured in centuries widens the breath.

**Act II shape**: texture over events. The daily life of a thirteenth-century scribe matters more than the dates of a war. Skip the kings. Stay with the rope-makers, the bakers, the salt-merchants.

**Voice to avoid**: history-as-conflict. Wars and uprisings are activating even when distant. If the topic has a war in it, hold the camera on the village before the war and let it stay there.

**Sample opening**: *"In the year twelve hundred and something, in a port town on the Baltic, a man was tying knots in rope."*

## Biography

**Examples**: a naturalist's quiet years, a poet's middle period, a philosopher's daily walks, a craftsman's apprenticeship.

**Why it works at bedtime**: a life observed from the outside is a kind of company.

**Act II shape**: stay in the quiet middle. Most lives are mostly quiet. The story is about the years between the famous events. Show the desk, the morning ritual, the walk.

**Voice to avoid**: triumph or tragedy. Avoid the dramatic arc. Avoid late-in-life illness. If the person is famous for one thing, the story is about everything else.

**Sample opening**: *"In a small house on the edge of a village, a woman who would later be famous was, at this point, simply sitting at a window."*

## Philosophy and ideas

**Examples**: Stoic equanimity, the ship of Theseus, what it is like to be a bat, Wittgenstein on language, the concept of slow time in Zen practice, Marcus Aurelius's nightly review.

**Why it works at bedtime**: a single well-handled idea is restful. Multiple ideas competing for attention is not.

**Act II shape**: concrete examples first. One idea, explored well. The abstract gesture comes after the listener has somewhere concrete to put it.

**Voice to avoid**: jargon, schools-of-thought summary, "philosopher X said while philosopher Y countered." Skip the lineage. Stay with the idea itself.

**Sample opening**: *"There is an old idea that the self is something like a ship that has all of its boards replaced, slowly, one at a time."*

## Place

**Examples**: a quiet harbor at dusk, a city at four in the morning, an alpine meadow in late summer, a Japanese garden in winter, a desert at the hour before dawn, a long train journey through northern terrain.

**Why it works at bedtime**: place stories are already sensory. They start in the right register and stay there.

**Act II shape**: imagery throughout. Substance can be the texture of the place itself, the small ecologies, the rhythms of the people who live there. Less argument, more attention.

**Voice to avoid**: travel-guide. The listener is not visiting. They are being put somewhere quiet to rest.

**Sample opening**: *"The harbor at this hour is almost empty. A single rope is tied to a single piling."*

## Craft and slow process

**Examples**: how a bell is cast, how paper is made by hand, how a violin is built, how cheese ages in a cave, how wine ferments, how a kiln is loaded.

**Why it works at bedtime**: process is inherently paced. The listener follows the rhythm of the doing.

**Act II shape**: the steps in order, with attention to the waiting between them. The waiting is the substance.

**Voice to avoid**: instructional. The listener is not going to do this. The story is about the doing itself, from a respectful distance.

**Sample opening**: *"Casting a bell takes a long time. Most of the time is waiting."*

## Genres to handle with care or redirect

- **War, atrocity, violence** → redirect to the geography, the era's daily life, or the long peace afterward.
- **Illness and dying** → Stoic and contemplative angles can settle. Personal medical detail does not.
- **Active political conflict** → almost always activating. Suggest the longer history of the place instead.
- **Horror, suspense, thriller** → wrong shape entirely. Suggest natural history at the same setting.
- **Romance with stakes** → drama activates. Long companionate marriages in old age can work.
- **Sports as competition** → competition activates. The slow training, the off-season, the equipment, can work.

When in doubt, offer the listener two adjacent topics that hold the *texture* they wanted without the *activation*.
